**Conclusion**
Current regulatory frameworks for AI agents in 2024 are fragmented, relying heavily on existing data protection laws (e.g., GDPR) and emerging sector-specific or state-level mandates rather than a unified federal standard in the U.S. Compliance strategies prioritize data privacy, security certifications (e.g., SOC 2), and transparent governance structures. While federal activity is accelerating following recent Executive Orders, comprehensive national AI legislation remains pending.

**Regulatory Landscape**
*   **United States:** General federal AI regulations are currently absent, with compliance driven by local, state, and industry-specific laws. Notable developments include California’s proposed *Safe and Secure Innovation for Frontier Artificial Intelligence Models Act* (SB 1047) and SEC proposals regarding conflicts of interest for investment advisers. Copyrightability also remains a constraint, with courts affirming human authorship requirements for autonomously generated works.
*   **International Influence:** The EU AI Law and GDPR continue to influence global development, requiring built-in privacy protections and user agency over private data.
*   **Government Guidance:** Agencies like the GSA emphasize alignment with Executive Order 14179 and OMB memoranda, utilizing Enterprise Risk Management policies to define risk tolerance and accountability.

**Risk Mitigation Strategies**
*   **Governance:** Organizations are advised to establish transparent governance, including AI Safety Teams to assess use cases and risk rubrics to rank likelihood and impact.
*   **Security & Privacy:** Essential practices include SOC 2 compliance for cloud and machine learning development, ensuring vendors are classified correctly as data controllers or processors, and implementing "secure by design" systems.
*   **Monitoring:** Companies must continuously monitor new federal and state regulations to ensure alignment with existing laws while awaiting broader statutory updates.
